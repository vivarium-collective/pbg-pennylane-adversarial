## Where this came from

The section above was the conclusion of a discussion about whether this repo's adversarial-QML pipeline is scientifically novel
when applied to v2ecoli WCM data as-is (short answer: only narrowly — classical baselines already match/beat the QML classifier
on tabular WCM features, and the specific task tried (`number_of_oric` 2-vs-4) is a near-deterministic physiological threshold,
so there isn't much room for "does adversarial robustness differ" to be an interesting question). That raised a broader one:
**what would a genuinely useful/novel quantum-computing application to the v2ecoli WCM system look like**, beyond
classify-and-attack? This document is the answer, built from a full pass over every demo in PennyLane's official gallery
(207 pages, `https://pennylane.ai/demonstrations`, researched 2026-07-02), mapped against what's actually known about the WCM
domain from this repo, the existing classical `surrogate-modeling` investigation in `v2ecoli` (a residual-MLP trained on
`TransitionDataset` — continuous state transitions, held-out-trajectory split), and the WCM's own structure (a large stochastic
reaction-network simulation with named submodels for metabolism/FBA, transcription, translation, replication, chromosome
dynamics, etc., producing hive-partitioned Parquet listener output).

Two axes, per the framing that prompted this: **(A) modeling** — demos that introduce actual quantum-mechanical or
quantum-structural concepts not available classically — and **(B) optimization/speedup exploitation** — demos where a hybrid
quantum-classical architecture is doing real algorithmic work, not just relabeling a classical ML task with a quantum layer.

## A. Modeling use cases

### A1. _(dynamics modeling)_ Graph-structured dynamics learning as a structural rival to the classical MLP surrogate
**Demos:** `tutorial_qgrnn` (Quantum Graph Recurrent Neural Network — a recurrent, graph-structured variational circuit trained
to reproduce the time evolution generated by an *unknown* Hamiltonian), `tutorial_learning_dynamics_incoherently` (learning an
unknown quantum process from sequential/shadow measurement data rather than coherent access).

**Why it maps:** the existing `v2ecoli` surrogate is an unstructured residual MLP over `(X, Y)` transition pairs — it has no
notion that WCM's regulatory/metabolic network is a *graph* (genes/operons/reactions as nodes, regulatory or stoichiometric
edges). QGRNN's core idea is to bake a graph topology directly into the circuit that learns the system's time-evolution
generator, rather than learning an arbitrary function of the flattened feature vector. Applied to WCM: represent a slice of the
regulatory or metabolic network as a graph, and train a QGRNN-style circuit to reproduce transition dynamics on that graph —
producing a model whose learned coupling terms are interpretable as effective interaction strengths between genes/reactions,
which a black-box MLP surrogate cannot offer. This is the most direct, concrete extension of the surrogate-modeling investigation
this repo already touches — a legitimate second surrogate architecture to compare against the classical one, not a relabeled
classification task.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** a researcher running WCM parameter sweeps or "what if" scenarios gets a fast emulator that also explains
*which* gene/reaction couplings drove a predicted outcome ("raising this operon's expression rate is predicted to delay
replication initiation, and Y% of that effect traces to this specific regulatory edge") instead of an opaque number out of an
MLP.

**Why it's useful:** it gives the existing `v2ecoli` surrogate-modeling investigation a second, structurally-grounded
architecture to benchmark against the classical MLP — if it matches or beats the MLP on the same held-out-trajectory split while
also being interpretable, that's a real capability upgrade for anyone building on that surrogate (not just this repo), since the
MLP currently offers no way to attribute a prediction to specific biological structure.

### A2. _(molecular modeling)_ Multiscale quantum embedding for metabolic rate-constant refinement
**Demos:** `tutorial_dmet_embedding` (Density Matrix Embedding Theory — treats a small strongly-correlated fragment quantum
mechanically while the rest of the system stays classical), `tutorial_qdet_embedding` (Quantum Defect Embedding Theory, same
idea for solid-state point defects), `tutorial_vqe`/`tutorial_differentiable_HF`/`tutorial_adaptive_circuits` (VQE family for
computing ground-state energies of a molecular subsystem).

**Why it maps:** WCM's metabolism submodel runs on empirically-fit kinetic rate constants, not first-principles chemistry — it
has no mechanism to quantum-mechanically compute e.g. enzyme active-site binding energetics. DMET/QDET's whole premise —
classically treat 99% of a system, quantum-mechanically treat one small strongly-correlated fragment, and only exchange
boundary information between the two — is *exactly* the embedding pattern a WCM would need: leave the rest of the cell's
metabolism as-is, quantum-compute a correction to one enzyme's rate constant, feed it back into the classical stoichiometric
model. This is expensive and narrow in scope (one enzyme/reaction at a time, not "quantum-simulate the whole metabolome"), but
it is the one item in this whole document that is actual quantum chemistry doing something classical methods structurally
cannot (multi-reference/strongly-correlated electronic structure), rather than a quantum ML model doing a task a classical model
could also do.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** a WCM modeler who currently has to hand-fit a kinetic rate constant against experimental data (or accept
an uncertain literature value) could instead get that constant computed from first-principles electronic structure for the
relevant enzyme's active site — a number with physical grounding rather than a curve fit.

**Why it's useful:** it reduces WCM's reliance on empirical curve-fitting for specific well-characterized reactions, and opens a
path to interrogate enzyme variants/mutations at the chemistry level (e.g., predicting how a point mutation shifts binding
energy) without needing new wet-lab kinetics data for every variant — a genuine extension of what the whole-cell model can
represent, not just a faster way to fit what it already represents.

### A3. _(generative modeling)_ Born-rule generative modeling for rare-phenotype / stochastic-trajectory augmentation
**Demos:** `tutorial_qcbm` (Quantum Circuit Born Machine — variational circuit trained so its Born-rule measurement statistics
match a target probability distribution), `tutorial_QGAN` / `tutorial_quantum_gans` (quantum generator trained adversarially
against a classical discriminator).

**Why it maps:** WCM simulation is itself fundamentally stochastic (Gillespie-style reaction firing) and expensive to run at
scale, so rare-but-important phenotypes (replication failures, extreme division-time outliers, near-death trajectories) are
underrepresented in any real dataset pulled from it — exactly the kind of gap generative data augmentation is for. There's a
closer conceptual match here than with a generic image GAN: a QCBM's core mechanism (a circuit's measurement outcomes *are*
literally drawn from a Born-rule probability distribution) parallels the fact that WCM trajectories are themselves samples from
an underlying stochastic-kinetics distribution — both problems are "learn a sampler that matches a target discrete
distribution," not "learn a deterministic mapping." Practically: bin/discretize a slice of WCM observables (e.g., division time,
final `number_of_oric`, terminal mass), train a QCBM or quantum-GAN generator against the empirical distribution from real
trajectories, and use it to oversample the tails.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** anyone training a classifier or surrogate on WCM data — including this repo's own classifier — gets a
larger, better-balanced training set for rare events (replication failure, near-death trajectories) without needing to run more
expensive real WCM simulations in the hope of naturally encountering them.

**Why it's useful:** it's a shared data-availability fix, not a one-off analysis — it directly improves training data for every
downstream ML use of WCM data in this ecosystem (this repo's classifier, the `v2ecoli` surrogate, any future model), which is a
recurring bottleneck given how expensive a real WCM run is to produce.

### A4. _(compression)_ Tensor-network compression of correlated WCM state
**Demos:** `tutorial_mps`, `tutorial_tensor_network_basics`, `tutorial_How_to_simulate_quantum_circuits_with_tensor_networks`
(PennyLane's MPS/tensor-network-backed `default.tensor` device).

**Why it maps:** a full WCM state is counts over thousands of correlated molecular species — storing/querying large ensembles of
full trajectories is itself a data-engineering cost this repo already works around (the classifier subsamples to 90/30 rows
because the circuit's forward pass isn't batched). Matrix-product-state representations are built exactly for compactly
representing high-dimensional, correlated-but-not-fully-entangled distributions. This one doesn't require running anything on
a QPU — PennyLane's tensor-network simulator device is a classical technique with a quantum-computing pedigree, offered here
because it's a legitimately different tool for the same "WCM state is too big to handle naively" problem the rest of this repo
has been solving with PCA.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** someone analyzing large ensembles of WCM trajectories (many lineage seeds/generations) could work with a
compact representation of the full correlated state instead of hitting the same "subsample to 90/30 rows because nothing scales"
wall this repo already hit on the `number_of_oric` run.

**Why it's useful:** it's a general-purpose fix to a bottleneck that's already shown up twice in this repo (the chunk-3
investigation's PCA fix, and the small subsample used for the real WCM classification run) — rather than another per-task
workaround, it addresses the underlying "WCM state is high-dimensional and correlated" problem once.

### Weaker modeling fits (researched, mentioned for completeness)
`tutorial_xas` (time-domain X-ray absorption spectroscopy simulation) and the broader Hamiltonian-simulation/pulse-programming
cluster (`tutorial_pulse_programming101`, `tutorial_odegen`, `ahs_aquila`, `oqc_pulse`) are physically about continuous-time
evolution under a *known, physical* Hamiltonian (spectroscopy, hardware pulse control) — WCM's dynamics aren't naturally posed
as Hamiltonian evolution (no unitary/energy-conserving structure), so these don't map without a forced physical analogy. Listed
so they aren't silently dropped from the research pass, not recommended.

## B. Optimization / hybrid speedup use cases

### B1. _(screening)_ Quantum-kernel advantage pre-screening — before training, not after
**Demos:** `tutorial_kernel_based_training`, `tutorial_kernels_module`, `tutorial_huang_geometric_kernel_difference` (a metric
from Huang et al. 2021 that predicts, *before training*, whether a quantum kernel can plausibly outperform classical kernels on
a given dataset).

**Why it maps:** this is the most immediately actionable item in this document, precisely because of what we already found —
Random Forest matched or beat the QML classifier on the real `number_of_oric` task. `tutorial_huang_geometric_kernel_difference`
gives a cheap diagnostic (a geometric-difference statistic computed directly from data, no circuit training required) for
whether a quantum kernel *could* have an advantage on a given feature slice, before spending a full train/attack/retrain cycle
finding out empirically the way we just did. Concretely: before picking the next WCM classification/regression task to run
through this pipeline, run the geometric-difference screen first. If it says no advantage is plausible, that's a legitimate
reason to either pick a different task or lead with the classical baseline as the primary model.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** before committing to a multi-hour train/attack/retrain run, someone gets a fast yes/no signal on whether
a QML approach is even worth trying on their chosen WCM task/feature slice — the exact check that would have told us in advance
that Random Forest was likely to match or beat the QML classifier on the `number_of_oric` task.

**Why it's useful:** it turns this repo's "pick a task, run the whole pipeline, find out after the fact" workflow into a
"screen first, then decide" workflow — a direct, low-cost improvement to how the CLI/pipeline gets used to survey candidate WCM
tasks going forward.

### B2. _(optimization)_ QAOA/QUBO for combinatorial strain-design problems adjacent to WCM
**Demos:** `tutorial_qaoa_intro`, `tutorial_qaoa_maxcut`, `tutorial_QUBO`.

**Why it maps:** WCM's own metabolism submodel is a continuous LP (FBA), which QAOA doesn't touch — but a well-known adjacent
problem in metabolic engineering *is* combinatorial: finding the minimal set of gene/reaction knockouts that forces a desired
phenotype (already formulated as MILP/QUBO in the metabolic-engineering literature). This isn't native to the WCM simulation
loop itself, but it's a legitimate downstream optimization problem over WCM-derived data (which reactions matter, which
combinations of knockouts a WCM run predicts would achieve a target growth/replication phenotype) that QAOA is architecturally
suited for, unlike most of the QML classifier/regressor demos in this gallery.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** a strain-design/metabolic-engineering researcher gets a ranked list of minimal gene/reaction knockout
combinations predicted to achieve a target phenotype, computed directly from WCM-derived data, instead of manually reasoning
through combinatorial knockout choices or running a brute-force/greedy classical search.

**Why it's useful:** it extends the WCM ecosystem beyond passive simulate-and-observe into active design-space search — a
genuinely new capability (combinatorial optimization over WCM-predicted phenotypes), not just another way to analyze the same
simulation output the rest of this document works with.

### B3. _(estimation)_ Amplitude estimation — the one demo family with a *proven* speedup, and the closest fit to WCM's Monte Carlo core
**Demos:** `tutorial_intro_amplitude_amplification`; adjacent linear-system solvers `linear_equations_hhl_qrisp_catalyst`
(HHL) and `tutorial_vqls` (NISQ-friendly variational alternative) if any WCM submodel reduces to a large sparse linear solve
(e.g. steady-state chemical master equation).

**Why it maps:** WCM simulation is fundamentally repeated stochastic (Gillespie-style) simulation to estimate expectations —
time-to-replication-initiation, division-time distributions, etc. — which is Monte Carlo estimation. Quantum amplitude
estimation is one of the few *quadratic, proven* (not empirically-hoped-for) quantum speedups for exactly this class of
problem. This is the strongest theoretical "why quantum, not just why QML" story in this whole document, and also the most
speculative in practice: nothing in this repo or the demo gallery currently applies amplitude estimation to a biological
stochastic simulation, and it would need real hardware (or a very generous simulator budget) to realize the speedup rather than
just the algorithm's structure.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect (if it ever becomes practical):** someone waiting on a WCM Monte Carlo estimate — e.g., a division-time
distribution over many stochastic runs — would get that estimate to the same statistical precision in roughly the square root
of the simulation runs, i.e., meaningfully faster turnaround for the same confidence.

**Why it's useful:** WCM's actual computational bottleneck *is* repeated stochastic simulation, so a proven quadratic speedup
here is the highest-leverage item in this whole document if it ever becomes practical. Today's honest usefulness is scoping and
awareness, not a deliverable — see the caveats section below on why this is gated on hardware that doesn't exist for this yet.

### B4. _(pattern-discovery)_ Barren-plateau diagnostics — explaining, not just avoiding, our own chunk-3 finding
**Demos:** `tutorial_barren_plateaus`, `tutorial_barren_gadgets`, `tutorial_local_cost_functions`.

**Why it maps:** this repo's own `docs/investigation_chunk3_high_dim_scaling/` already found, empirically, that auto-raising
`num_layers` to match high-dimensional input made accuracy stick at chance even after 50 epochs — and PCA-reducing features
instead fixed it. That finding was treated as an empirical curiosity to route around. `tutorial_barren_plateaus`'s
gradient-variance-vs-qubit/depth sweep is a direct diagnostic for whether that was barren-plateau-induced vanishing gradients
specifically (a named, well-studied failure mode) rather than some other cause — running it against our chunk-3 sweep data
would upgrade "we observed this and worked around it" into "we can show why," and `tutorial_local_cost_functions`/
`tutorial_barren_gadgets` are concrete mitigation techniques (local cost functions, perturbative gadgets) to try instead of PCA
if we want to preserve full dimensionality on a future run.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** nobody outside the project directly experiences this — it's an internal diagnostic. Whoever
maintains/extends this repo's QML circuits gets a documented, quantitative explanation ("gradient variance collapses at N
qubits/depth D") instead of an anecdote.

**Why it's useful:** it turns an ad hoc empirical workaround (PCA, because auto-raising layers didn't train) into predictive
guidance — before choosing `num_qubits`/`num_layers` for the next WCM task, check where the plateau kicks in, rather than
discovering it by trial and error the way the chunk-3 investigation did.

### B5. _(calibration)_ Optimizer-technique transfer for classical WCM kinetic-parameter calibration
**Demos:** `tutorial_quantum_natural_gradient`, `tutorial_qnspsa`, `tutorial_rosalin`, `tutorial_spsa`.

**Why it maps — with a caveat:** WCM has hundreds of empirically-fit kinetic rate constants, and calibrating them is a hard,
high-dimensional, non-convex, expensive-to-evaluate optimization problem (each evaluation is a WCM run). This is a weaker,
indirect fit: the *quantum circuits* in these demos aren't relevant to WCM at all, but the *optimizer research* behind them
(natural-gradient geometry, measurement-frugal/doubly-stochastic gradient estimation for expensive-to-evaluate objectives) is
directly transferable as classical optimization strategy, independent of whether a quantum circuit is anywhere in the loop.
Flagging this honestly as technique-transfer, not a "use PennyLane on WCM" use case.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** whoever calibrates WCM's kinetic parameters against experimental data gets faster, more reliable
convergence on parameter fits — fewer expensive WCM re-runs needed to land on a good parameter set.

**Why it's useful:** this is the one item in the document that helps the *classical* WCM directly, independent of whether any
quantum circuit is ever built — a pure optimizer-algorithm transfer that improves an existing WCM maintenance workflow
(parameter calibration), valuable on its own regardless of whether any other item here gets built.

### B6. _(planning)_ Resource-estimation-first workflow before scaling any of the above
**Demos:** `tutorial_resource_estimation`, `re_how_to_use_pennylane_for_resource_estimation`, and the QSVT/GQSP
resource-estimation cluster.

**Why it maps:** every idea above (A1–A4, B1–B3) that involves an actual circuit gets harder, not easier, at real WCM scale
(thousands of species vs. the 25-feature/4-qubit slice this repo has tested so far). PennyLane's resource-estimation tooling
(qubit/gate/T-count estimates ahead of running anything) is a cheap way to sanity-check feasibility of, e.g., a QGRNN over a
100-node regulatory subgraph, before committing engineering time — analogous to what the chunk-3 investigation did empirically
after the fact, but done as a planning step instead.

### Why is this useful/What this means
**Real-world effect:** before anyone spends engineering time building A1, A2, A3, B2, or B3 at real WCM scale, they get a
qubit/gate-count estimate telling them whether it's even feasible on available simulators/hardware.

**Why it's useful:** it's a cheap, low-risk planning gate applied *before* any other item in this document is greenlit —
protects engineering time across the whole roadmap this document lays out, rather than discovering infeasibility only after
building and running the thing, which is what happened with chunk-3's original auto-raise-layers approach.

## Recommended near-term shortlist (ranked)

1. **B1 — quantum-kernel advantage pre-screening.** Cheapest to try, directly answers "should we even expect QML to win here"
   before the next full pipeline run, and would have told us something useful about the `number_of_oric` task in advance.
2. **A1 — QGRNN-style graph-structured surrogate.** Most direct extension of work already in flight (the `v2ecoli`
   surrogate-modeling investigation + this repo's adversarial-attack infra), and the strongest "structurally different from
   the classical baseline, not just relabeled" story.
3. **B4 — barren-plateau diagnostics on the chunk-3 data.** Data already exists (`docs/investigation_chunk3_high_dim_scaling/`);
   this is analysis of results we already have, not a new experiment.
4. **A3 — QCBM/QGAN generative augmentation for rare phenotypes.** Conceptually clean fit (Born-rule sampler ↔ stochastic
   kinetics sampler) and addresses a real WCM-data-availability problem (rare trajectories are expensive to obtain).
5. **A2 — DMET/QDET embedding for one metabolic rate constant.** Highest scientific novelty ceiling (actual quantum chemistry
   doing something classically hard) but narrowest scope and highest setup cost — a good "stretch" investigation, not a first
   step.
6. **B2 / B3 — QAOA strain-design, amplitude estimation.** Real and well-motivated, but both are one level removed from the WCM
   simulation loop itself (downstream combinatorial design problem; long-run theoretical speedup story) rather than something
   to prototype against the data we already have on hand.

## Weak fits — researched but not recommended

Large parts of the gallery are architecturally irrelevant to WCM and are noted here so the research pass is documented as
complete, not silently trimmed: proven-speedup algorithms aimed at pure computer-science problems with no WCM analogue (Shor's
factoring, Grover search on unstructured databases, quantum teleportation, QFT/period-finding); the entire fault-tolerance/QEC
cluster (stabilizer codes, lattice surgery, magic-state distillation) and hardware-platform primers (neutral atoms, trapped
ions, superconducting qubits, photonics) — these are about *building better quantum computers*, not applications; and
compilation/circuit-transformation tooling (ZX-calculus, KAK decomposition, ZNE error mitigation) which matters once any of the
above is actually running on hardware, but isn't itself a modeling or optimization use case.

## Honest caveats

- **Everything here currently runs on `lightning.qubit`-style statevector simulators, including this repo's own classifier.**
  No real quantum hardware is in the loop anywhere. That means every "speedup" claim above (B2, B3 especially) is aspirational,
  not realized on the stack we actually have — the near-term, honestly deliverable value from this list is in **A1, A3, A4, B1,
  B4**: structural/representational novelty and diagnostic tooling, not wall-clock speed.
- **This mirrors the caveat already raised about the current classifier work**: swapping in a "quantum" technique doesn't
  automatically make a use case scientifically novel if a classical method would do the same job — the shortlist above was
  ranked with that filter already applied (favoring items where the quantum structure is doing something a classical
  MLP/LP/GAN genuinely can't, not just a relabeled version of it).
- None of this has been prototyped yet — this document is a scoping/research artifact, not an implementation plan.
